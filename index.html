Four Views: Our initial thoughts on the topic of Gun Violence

Beth's View

"We’re not talking about the mass shootings making headlines every week. Homicides, suicides, and accidents kill an average of 36,000 people each year; that’s approximately 100 killed every single day then add more than 100,000 who are injured on top of that. Firearms are the second leading cause of death for American children and teens. In an average month, 52 American women are shot to death by an intimate partner, and many more are injured. The numbers are staggering, unbelievable, shocking, disturbing, and difficult to relate to. Our project’s goal is to bridge the gap between the numbers and our brains through visualizations."

Carol's View

"Gun violence impacts everyone in the United States in someway. A quick google search of just the words, Gun Violence, brings up many topics for discussion, such as the impact of mass shootings, suicide rates versus homicide, the number of guns in the US, the number of deaths in the US, mental health issues, regulation conflicts, and even constitutional rights of the 2nd amendment.  Is there a solution?  We don't have that answer, but we do believe, better visualization of the issue may bring better understanding, which can lead to creative solutions." 

Enzo's View

"A review of weapons offenses recorded by the FBI Uniform Crime Reporting Program (UCR) was conducted. Note data from Nebraska was unavailable from the FBI UCR. The total number of weapons offenses from 2000 to 2017 found that California, Texas, North Carolina, New Jersey and New York lead the list as the top five states in said order. Review of the victim count as recorded by the Mother Jones Mass Shooting data set found that the top five states based on victim count were Nevada, California, Texas, Florida and Colorado. Lastly a review of the total number of weapons registered as of 2019 found the top five states with the most weapons registered to be Texas, Florida, California, Virginia, and Pennsylvania."

Ian's View

"It is no secret that mass shootings are far more common in America than any other first world country. Why this is, is a question with a complicated answer. Conclusions: What We Now Think

Beth's Summary

"There are three things banks can do immediately, today: Just like money laundering flags, create flags for gun violence Purchasing multiple weapons in a short period of time Purchasing assault related accessories like bump stocks, body armor, high capacity magazines, large quantities of bullets, and types of especially damaging ammunition Most weapons are purchased using credit, make all guns and related equipment purchases cash only Stop lending and financial products for companies that manufacture semi-automatic firearms."

Carol's Summary

Gun violence was not an easy topic for learning or expressing what I have learned while researching the topic. Guns and violence bring out strong emotions and evoke a strong undercurrent of fear in daily society that was emphasized while working on this project. I believed prior to working on this topic that a multi-faceted approach is the only way bring a solution. I still believe any solution must include a reduction in the power of the NRA to control our society and our officials, improved mental health care access for all, reduced racial and economic disparities and improved "common sense" gun controls that reduce the ability of desperate people to have access to guns when what they need is help. Learnings from the project are: Data is never easy but it is enlightening, you can learn a lot from simple google searches and breaking down tasks into the smallest bit possible.

Enzo's Summary

"While there does appear to be some correlation among weapons offenses, registered weapons and mass shooting victim counts with what are conventionally known to be states with greater population sizes, because of political agendas potentially influencing data sources any viable evidence relating the value could not be derived. Given the availability of additional resources and data source material, data relating to state legislation may provide further insight."

Ian's Summary

"Part of my role in this project was to try to locate guns in America. I was shocked to find that the most accurate data I could find was just a percentage of gun ownership by state. In order to at least try to solve the mass shooting problem in America, there needs to be more accurate collection of data for gun owners. Through these records and maybe some internet monitoring I still believe that this mass shooting problem can be resolved. The question is how much privacy Americans are willing to give up in hopes for making America lose the title of first world country with highest number of mass shootings."
